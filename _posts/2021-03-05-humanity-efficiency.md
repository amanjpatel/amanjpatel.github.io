Humanity and efficiency often seem to be at odds with one another. 

Arts and culture, the de facto definition of “the humanities,” are innately inefficient. Resources are spent on developing entertaining creations that do nothing to satisfy our basic needs.

Most of our leisure, of course, is inefficient too. It’s not rest and relaxation in itself that is problematic--giving ourselves time off is an important part of keeping our productivity near peak. It’s what we do in that time that’s inefficient. 

We spend lots of time locomoting from place to place during our off hours, whether it be the park or a campground or a Carribean island. Often, we could experience something similarly fun without needing to go that far.

We could also use our mental resources more optimally during leisure time. If we’re watching a fictional TV show, why not watch a more informative video instead, where we learn valuable and practical knowledge instead of character drama? If we’re playing an intense video game, why not use those same mental resources to solve a difficult practical problem instead? 

We very much enjoy communication inefficiency. Small talk is entirely unnecessary and adds no novel insights to anybody’s life, but it serves as the foundation for many of our interpersonal relationships. Participating in small talk well is a necessary component of appearing friendly and sociable. 

Efficiency can appear to be the antithesis of these important elements of the human experience. A world without arts might be labeled as “unlivable” or “dystopian,” evoking images of a bleak and joyless environment. Leisure without movement might not feel like leisure at all; certainly, the pandemic has given us all the desire to “get out of the house,” since free time at home isn’t quite fulfilling. Optimizing leisure for solving practical problems might conjure some strange looks, if not concerns from family members. Conversations and meetings without gossip, banter, or remarks on the weather would likely seem alien. 

Despite our cultural rejection of the struggle for efficiency, our economic lives reflect the opposite trend. Companies are constantly competing with each other to find the most efficient means of production, and employees devour literature about how to maximize their personal productivity. We abhor inefficiencies in corporate bureaucracies or governments.

The purpose of any technology is to increase efficiency, and this point is driven home by new AI systems that offer to automate not only our physical actions, but also our thinking and cognition—doing these tasks several orders of magnitude faster than we can.

How do we merge our social distaste for efficiency with our economic love for it? 

The answer might lie in the nature of efficiency itself. Efficiency in some areas allows us the freedom to be inefficient in others, and vice versa. The less time we spend on earning our income needs, the more time we can devote to creating and experiencing arts and culture. The more we automate our rational thinking and decision-making, the more we can spend on casual chats and social time.

Time spent loafing in the woods gives rise to deep thought and new ideas that would otherwise be masked by the demands of a hectic life. The arts give us a renewed sense of motivation and drive. 

Humanity is not the opposite of efficiency; it’s just a complement. There’s a symbiotic relationship between humanity and efficiency that we should recognize, before we get the urge to throw out one in favor of the other. Maximum humanity, whatever that entails, is not achieved by minimum efficiency. It’s not achieved by maximum efficiency either. Instead, it peaks somewhere in the middle.

Our cultural instinct to treat them as opposites might prevent us from reaching to higher heights on either.
