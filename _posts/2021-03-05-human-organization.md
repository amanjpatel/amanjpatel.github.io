As the title says, human organization is the cause of and solution to all problems. Organization, in this context, is a collective term for social roles, relationships between roles, patterns of communication, and behavioral constraints that define our individual dynamics in a wider society.

Let’s take the big names. Climate change? A matter of not being able to get our act together. Widespread pandemics? The same thing. Wars? Yup. Economic sluggishness? Inherently tied to policy.

The same happens for problems on a personal scale: work drama, family fights, even that pothole on the way home are all consequences of our flawed structures of relating to others. 

Crucially, these problems are not necessarily the fault of any particular humans within these structures. In the context of their daily lives, most people are just trying to fill the local roles they have. Oil company employees are stuck in a position where making more sustainable decisions might not be feasible, and your manager at work is just doing what she’s been told by her boss. Very few people actually intend to cause problems that might harm others--instead, we’re all just following along with our local eddy in the broader flow of humanity, a flow that’s tightly constrained by the pipe maze of social structures and collective norms that we’ve built. 

A friend once put it like this: our problems are caused by good people in oppressive structures.

The flip side of this, of course, is that better human organization can help us solve or prevent these problems. All the problems named above could be eliminated with better-adapted models of relating to each other. 

This might seem quite obvious for the macro-scale problems, but it’s true of the micro-scale ones as well: decentralized management structures have been shown to radically increase employee productivity and well-being, and many family fights could be avoided with wiser communication habits.

Our main challenge, then, is recognizing the deficiencies in our current organizational structures, and amending them. We don’t seem to have any problem recognizing these deficiencies--most people on the street would be able to give you a laundry list of everything they think is wrong with how we relate to each other, and where we’re headed.

The limiting factor preventing us from making progress on our flaws is not our knowledge of them, but rather our knowledge of each other. 

Many polls show that people don’t have a good understanding of how many other people share their view. If we all knew what each of us thinks about issues, we might be surprised at how many things we agree on. This information might give us the clarity needed to make swift fixes to the organizational flaws we agree on, and spend more time thinking through the ones we don’t.

If we could gather this knowledge about our collective opinions on issues quickly and conveniently, we could easily work up the momentum to change both formal and informal structures. We could create agile feedback loops between individuals and institutions, so that we can shape our institutions to be ones that we’re proud to live under. We could amend our organizational flaws rapidly and en masse, facilitating an energetic paradigm of continuous improvement.

We can create an immensely beautiful future if we just improve the flow of information between us, and I’m excited to be a part of this endeavor.
