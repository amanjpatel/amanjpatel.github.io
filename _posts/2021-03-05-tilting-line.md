When thinking about all the problems that the world faces, it's easy to be overwhelmed. An  immense amount of suffering occurs every single second, and if we aren't careful, there might be even more in the future. Many of us build our life goals in the context of this darkness, seeking to brighten it as much as we can--but framing our goals this way can quickly lead to a feeling of being swallowed or lost. 

I've found that a more useful model for thinking about my life goals is to tilt the slope of our cosmos's line upwards.

You can choose to assign a concrete meaning to this line, but you don't need to. It's okay if it represents a poorly-defined or nebulous concept--it doesn’t have to stand for a rigorous philosophical construct. When I imagine this line, I think of the total well-being in the world. In this context, I don't even try to define well-being. There's a time and a place where it's important to be very precise about definitions, but it's unnecessary here.

So now you have a line. You might have an idea of what it looked like in the past, or you might not. Maybe our line is heading downwards right now, or maybe not. Maybe it's accelerating in a particular direction, or maybe it's behaving very erratically. It doesn't really matter. 

Our goal is to tilt this line upward, wherever it is right now. We're not trying to cause an immediate jump to a higher level, or to spark a rapid acceleration upwards. Those are often unrealistic goals that lead only to sadness and frustration. No matter what shallow motivational speakers might have you think, nobody can cause a large and immediate change alone. Our impact is composed of many small but immediate changes, building up into a large change over time. 

Each time we add a positive contribution, the line tilts upwards, even if imperceptibly so. All contributions are meaningful, even if they're not all of the same magnitude. Some actions might have downstream effects that inspire others or catalyze more change. Some actions might instead create a short-lived bump in joy. On average, it's simpler to average these out, and think of each action as a change in the slope of our line. 

If everyone increases the slope of our line by just a little, we can achieve very rapid change, and we'll be on our way to a much better place in no time.
