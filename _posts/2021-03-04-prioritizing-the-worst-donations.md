Many people in the effective altruism community focus their time on researching the better places to donate money. This is a tremendously important task: there is a very large amount of money donated to charitable causes each year, but much of it goes to charities with very little real impact.

There's a fine line, though, between trying to find better places to donate money, and searching for the best place. We must be very wary of this line, so that we don't embark on fruitless endeavors. 

At some point, the marginal returns on charity evaluation and donation-effectiveness research dip below the costs of that research. I don't think we've reached that place yet, given the vast amount of money in charity and the vast amount of evaluations that have yet to be completed.

Instead, the potential problem I see is in prioritization: is more charity research the best use of our time, knowledge, and money right now? There might still be much charity research to be done, all with relatively high marginal returns. Is it worth spending your time doing that charity research, instead of campaigning to shift donations to the charities we already know are better? Maybe not. We might not need to find the best places to put out resources--rather, it might be better to focus on shifting donations away from the worst places, even if they don't ultimately go to the "best" ones. We can still continue charity evaluation work on the side, but we have plenty of information right now to shift the vast mass of ineffective donations to a better place.

We can't let perfect be the enemy of good. We should always be striving to find better options, of course, but it's important to apply our current knowledge to move resources to what we already know is better than the status quo. We might do this periodically or continuously, but at some point we must throw our efforts behind campaigning for change.
